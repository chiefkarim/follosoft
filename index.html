Snow Plow Maintenance Guide: Keep Your Fleet Running All Winter

A complete guide to preventive maintenance, daily inspections, and troubleshooting, so your equipment performs when it matters most.

FolloSoft Team · April 15, 2026 · 10 min read

It's 3 AM, snow is piling up, and your crew is stuck. A hydraulic line failed, and they are waiting for a replacement. Your clients are counting on you, and your workers are out in the brutal conditions.

why? Because no one checked the equipment before. A solid maintenance program helps your crew get back home faster. Cost reduction starts with maintaining your equipment.

Most equipment failures are preventable with a solid maintenance program. This guide walks you through everything you need to stay ahead of breakdowns. from daily checks to end-of-season storage.

Why Preventive Maintenance Pays for Itself

Be proactive, not reactive. Sounds like cliché self-help book advice, right? But how many times has a spare hose saved you in the middle of a storm?

Protecting your profit starts with keeping your operations running when it really matters. And the way you do that is by putting in the boring, unpleasant work of maintenance.

Well-maintained equipment lasts up to 40% longer and saves you serious money over time. Solid preventive programs can cut in-season breakdowns by up to 60%. Emergency repairs during a storm routinely cost 3–5 times as much as scheduled service, not to mention towing, overtime, and pissed-off clients.

When your team sticks to regular preventive routines and checkups, jobs finish faster, everyone gets home earlier, and you avoid those ugly surprises that eat into your profit margin. Scaling your operation while keeping high standards is hard enough, it becomes nearly impossible if you can't trust your equipment.

Pre-Season Inspection Checklist

Don't get caught off guard when the first snow flies. Before a single flake falls, put every piece of equipment through a thorough inspection. starting is eearly fall gives you plenty of time to order any parts or get repairs done without panicking.

For each plow unit, use this checklist:

  1. Hydraulic system: Make sure to inspect all hoses, fittings, cylinders, and fluid levels. Look for cracks, bulges, or wet spots on hoses. A small hose issue now beats a midnight failure later.
  2. Blades and cutting edges: Measure wear. Replace any cutting-edge worn past 50%. It's not overkill, it's security.
  3. Electrical connections: Test all lights, strobes, beacons, and wiring harnesses. Corrosion happens the most after off-season storage.
  4. Mounting hardware: Check frame alignment, mounting bolts, and torque specs. Loose mounts cause uneven wear and dangerous safety issues fast.
  5. Spreader components: Inspect chains, spinner plates, augers, and vibrators on salt and sand spreaders. You will often find frozen or worn parts here. Catch them early and enjoy a smooth snow removal.
  6. Tires and undercarriage: Check tire pressure, tread depth, wheel bearings, and suspension components. These parts take a beating over time.
  7. Safety systems: Test backup cameras, alarms, mirrors, and any GPS tracking units. These parts are your eyes and ears in low-visibility conditions under heavy weather. You don't want them to fail you.
Tip: Create a standardized inspection form for your team. Consistent documentation makes it easy to spot recurring issues across your fleet before they become expensive headaches. (You can print the checklist at the end of the blog.)

Daily and Weekly Checks During the Season

Once the season starts, your equipment takes a beating. Daily checks catch small problems before they become big ones.

After Every Shift

  • Wash everything: Salt and brine are extremely corrosive. A quick pressure wash after each use dramatically extends equipment life.
  • Grease all pivot points and moving parts
  • Check hydraulic fluid levels: low fluid means there's a leak somewhere
  • Visually inspect cutting edges for chips, cracks, or uneven wear
  • Look under the truck for fresh fluid drips or hanging components

Weekly

  • Inspect hydraulic hoses more carefully, feel for soft spots, and check fittings
  • Check all bolts and fasteners for tightness
  • Test all lighting and electrical systems
  • Review tire pressure and condition
  • Top off all fluids

Blade and Cutting Edge Maintenance

Your cutting edge is a wear item; it's supposed to wear down. The key is replacing it at the right time.

Signs it's time to replace

  • Cutting edge is worn past the halfway point
  • Uneven wear pattern (indicates alignment issues)
  • Visible cracks or chunks missing
  • You're hearing metal-on-pavement scraping (the moldboard is hitting the ground)

Tips for longer blade life

  • Match your cutting edge material to your primary surface. Carbide for asphalt, steel for gravel, polyurethane for decorative surfaces.
  • Adjust plow shoes and skids properly to control cutting edge contact pressure.
  • Flip reversible edges when one side wears down.
  • Store spare edges on-site so your crew can swap them without returning to the shop.

Blade alignment matters: A misaligned blade wears unevenly and puts extra stress on the hydraulic system. Check alignment whenever you replace an edge.

Hydraulic System Care

The hydraulic system is the heart of your plow. When it fails, the plow stops moving.

Keep it healthy

  • Use the manufacturer-recommended hydraulic fluid. Mixing fluid types causes seal damage.
  • Change hydraulic fluid and filters according to the manufacturer's schedule, or at a minimum, once per season.
  • Inspect hoses before every shift. Replace any hose that shows cracking, bulging, abrasion, or wet spots.
  • Keep spare hoses and fittings on your trucks. A $30 hose and 15 minutes of shop time beats a $500 emergency roadside repair.
  • Bleed air from the system after any hose replacement or fluid top-off.

Watch for these warning signs

  • Slow or jerky plow movement
  • Unusual noises from the pump
  • Fluid that looks milky (water contamination) or dark (overheating)
  • Spongy or unresponsive controls

Common Problems and Quick Fixes

Even with great maintenance, issues happen. Here are the most common mid-season problems and what to do:

Problem Likely Cause Quick Fix
Plow won't angle Low hydraulic fluid or blown hose Check fluid, inspect hoses
Cutting edge wears unevenly Misaligned blade or worn plow shoes Adjust shoes, check alignment
Spreader chain jams Salt bridging or frozen material Break up material, check chain tension
Electrical flickering Corroded connectors Clean and apply dielectric grease
Plow bounces on pavement Worn trip springs or shock absorbers Inspect and replace springs
Hydraulic fluid leaking Worn seals or damaged hose Replace seals/hose, check fittings

Keep a small emergency kit on each truck: spare hoses, fittings, hydraulic fluid, cutting-edge bolts, electrical connectors, and basic hand tools. Many mid-shift breakdowns can be resolved in the field.

End-of-Season Storage

How you store your equipment in spring determines how it performs next winter.

  1. Deep clean everything: Remove all salt, brine, and debris. Pay special attention to undercarriages, electrical connectors, and hydraulic fittings.
  2. Retract all hydraulic cylinders and relieve system pressure.
  3. Coat exposed metal with a rust inhibitor or light oil film.
  4. Disconnect and remove batteries or connect to a maintenance charger.
  5. Store indoors if possible. If outdoor storage is unavoidable, use quality covers and elevate equipment off bare ground.
  6. Document everything: Note any issues observed during the season, parts that need replacement, and upcoming service intervals.

That last point is critical. The notes you take in April save you hours of guesswork in October.

Build a Maintenance Culture

The best maintenance programs aren't just checklists. They're habits your whole team follows.

  • Assign responsibility: Each operator should be accountable for their equipment's daily checks.
  • Track everything: Use a logbook, spreadsheet, or fleet management software to record inspections, repairs, and part replacements. When you can see maintenance history at a glance, you make better decisions about repairs vs. replacement.
  • Schedule, don't react: Block time for preventive maintenance before the season and during slow periods. Waiting for a breakdown is always more expensive.
  • Stock critical spares: Identify the parts that fail most often and keep them in inventory.

Good documentation also protects you during disputes, insurance claims, and equipment resale. A truck with a complete maintenance history is worth significantly more than one without.
